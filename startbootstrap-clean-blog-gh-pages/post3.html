The Jubalaires

Rap's first artists.

Posted by Randall Harris on November 16th, 2022

Who were they, how were they influenced by myth, and how were they culturally significant?

While spirituals were, by nature, uncommercialized, the combination of The Great Migration as well as the rise in mainstream popularity of African American music meant that the nation was experiencing black culture at an anomalous rate compared to a mere few decades earlier in the nineteenth century. With the commercialization of the spiritual came a new breed of American music, gospels. Gospels were usually centered on the New Testament stories while still holding many of the values of Old Testament spirituals. Ideas of hope, saviors, love, and even vengeance were all very popular and topical themes for early gospel groups to work with; along with the added benefit of selling extremely well.

One such group that emerged in the mid 1940’s was The Jubalaires, a group that started out as spiritual singer/songwriters performing under the name Royal Harmony Singers until 1936 when they rebranded as a gospel/rhythm and blues group. This rebranding led to a change in style and audience, the object of interest that remained intact, however, was their use of rhythm in their singing. The Jubalaires tended to fall into a sort-of spoken-word style that bounced along with the backing rhythm. This turned out to be a massive boon for the group, as it landed them at number ten on the national rhythm and blues charts in 1942, as well as securing spots in film, the record industry, and Arthur Godfrey’s CBS radio show to name only a few of their achievements. Along with their success, The Jubalaires use of rhythm, spoken word, and lyrics with a clear “flow” and pattern, had set the gears in motion to form the early stages of rap.
